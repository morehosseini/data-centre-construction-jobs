# Final Evidence Dossier: Warehouse and Fulfilment Centre Construction and Operations Jobs per Capex and Floor Area

## 1) Executive Summary
The global logistics real estate sector is undergoing a profound structural transformation driven by e-commerce penetration and the aggressive integration of autonomous robotics. For urban planners and economic development agencies, evaluating the opportunity cost of capital—how identical capital investments (e.g., US$100 million) perform across different industrial built assets—is critical. 

This dossier synthesises empirical evidence on capital-to-labour ratios and spatial employment density. The findings reveal a stark divergence between the construction and operational phases of modern logistics hubs. During construction, capital expenditure reliably generates high temporary employment (approximately 400–550 direct jobs per US$100 million). However, in the operational phase, the sector is experiencing severe "spatial labour decay." Driven by capital-for-labour substitution (automation and robotics), modern multi-story fulfilment centres operate at a spatial density of 185 to 235 square metres (sqm) per Full-Time Equivalent (FTE) worker, fundamentally contradicting legacy planning guidelines that assumed 70 to 95 sqm per FTE. Furthermore, while logistics facilities yield higher gross operational headcounts than hyperscale data centres (which act as low-employment "rentier assets"), their net causal local employment impact is often diluted by the cannibalisation of local retail jobs and transient labour leakage during construction.

## 2) Assessment of the Current Search Approach and its Coverage
The search approach utilised a multi-hop structural snowballing protocol to bridge the gap between theoretical macroeconomic models and empirical project-level data. 
*   **Coverage Strengths:** The search successfully captured authoritative grey literature (e.g., UK Homes and Communities Agency [HCA] guidelines, British Property Federation [BPF] reports, and Louisiana Economic Development [LED] audits) alongside peer-reviewed academic literature across Transport Geography, Operations Management, and Regional Economics. This cross-disciplinary approach successfully identified the "Density Illusion"—the conflation of site footprint with Gross Floor Area (GFA) in multi-story robotics facilities.
*   **Coverage Limitations:** The evidence base remains heavily reliant on ex-ante promotional claims and legally binding but forward-looking Cooperative Endeavor Agreements (CEAs). There is a systemic deficit of ex-post longitudinal audits that verify realised headcount 5–10 years post-launch. Additionally, independent, non-vendor-funded operational baselines for data centres remain sparse.

## 7) Methods / Approaches Synthesis
The evaluation of capital-intensive built assets relies on a disparate set of analytical traditions:
*   **Macroeconomic Input-Output (I-O) Models:** Frameworks like IMPLAN and RIMS II are universally used to estimate indirect and induced employment from construction capex. While highly mature, they assume static supply chains and often ignore displacement effects and transient labour leakage.
*   **Spatial Econometrics & System Dynamics:** Used in academic literature to map cross-border labour leakage and the nonlinear, time-delayed feedback loops of construction labour markets (e.g., the "employment cliff" post-construction).
*   **Planning and Spatial Heuristics:** Municipalities rely on Employment Density Multipliers (e.g., sqm/FTE) applied to Gross Floor Area (GFA). These heuristics are highly susceptible to temporal degradation as automation decouples physical space from human labour.
*   **Ex-Post Evaluative Audits:** Longitudinal, retrospective econometric audits are the most rigorous method for validating net causal job creation against ex-ante tax incentive promises, though they are severely underutilised in practice.

## 8) Applications, Outcomes, and Adoption Synthesis
*   **The Construction Baseline:** Industrial capital expenditure behaves uniformly across asset classes. Whether building a warehouse or a data centre, construction yields roughly 400–550 direct jobs per US$100 million invested.
*   **The Automation Paradox & Labour Decay:** Modern logistics facilities are adopting multi-story architectures (e.g., Amazon Johnston's 3.8M sq ft GFA on a 20-acre footprint) to house Automated Storage and Retrieval Systems (AS/RS). This capital-for-labour substitution has driven spatial density down from historical norms of 70–95 sqm/FTE to 185–235 sqm/FTE.
*   **The Equipment Skew:** In advanced manufacturing (e.g., semiconductor fabs) and data centres, up to 80% of capex is spent on imported specialised equipment rather than structural building materials, drastically lowering the local construction jobs generated *per dollar* compared to warehouses.
*   **Net vs. Gross Employment:** The introduction of hyperscale e-commerce fulfilment centres frequently results in near-zero *net* local job growth, as the gross jobs created inside the facility cannibalise existing jobs in local retail and traditional warehousing.

## 9) Quality, Governance, Risk, and Limitations Synthesis
*   **Risk of Bias:** Developer-commissioned economic impact statements carry a high risk of bias, frequently conflating gross jobs with net local employment. Official government announcements carry moderate bias (reporting anticipated maximums). Academic and municipal audits offer the lowest bias but suffer from temporal lag.
*   **Metric Distortion (GFA vs. Footprint):** A systemic risk in municipal planning is calculating employment density using the ground-level foundation footprint rather than the multi-story GFA, which artificially inflates projected job density.
*   **Labour Leakage:** Massive capital injections in suburban/rural areas rapidly exhaust local labour pools, necessitating imported, transient construction workers. This severely dilutes the local economic capture of the construction phase.
*   **Algorithmic Obfuscation:** Corporate entities frequently shroud workforce deployment data and algorithmic efficiency gains in confidentiality, making it difficult for auditors to track the longitudinal decay of human labour post-automation.

## 11) Research Gaps and Next Steps
1.  **Ex-Post Longitudinal Audits:** There is a critical need for retrospective audits comparing projected peak headcounts announced during ground-breaking with realised annualised FTEs 3 to 5 years post-launch, particularly after automation retrofits.
2.  **Data Centre Operational Baselines:** The evidence base lacks empirical, non-industry-funded municipal audits explicitly linking data centre capital investment to realised permanent operational FTEs.
3.  **Quantification of Labour Leakage:** Future research must quantify the geographic origin of construction workers for hyperscale projects to determine the true net-local retention of construction payrolls.
4.  **Next Steps:** Execute targeted searches for tax-increment financing (TIF) audit reports and OSHA compliance logs to verify true on-site daily headcounts independent of corporate public relations.
