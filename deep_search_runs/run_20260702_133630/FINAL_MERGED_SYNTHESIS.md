# Final Evidence Dossier: Data-Centre Local Workforce Capture, Incentives Cost Per Job, and Ex-Post Audits

## 1) Executive Summary
The rapid global proliferation of hyperscale data centres has exposed a profound structural misalignment in contemporary economic development and public finance. Traditional economic development frameworks assume that massive capital expenditure (capex) generates proportionate local employment and supply-chain agglomeration. However, empirical evidence demonstrates that data centres represent an anomalous asset class characterised by a "capital-labour sparsity paradox." They require multi-billion-dollar upfront investments and monopolise local utility infrastructure (power and water) while generating a steep employment cliff post-construction, yielding minimal permanent operational jobs.

This dossier synthesises the evidence surrounding three critical accountability gaps. First, it highlights the failure of traditional Input-Output (I-O) economic multipliers due to severe supply-chain leakage; the vast majority of data-centre capex is spent on imported, highly specialised hardware, bypassing local economies. Second, it benchmarks the extreme fiscal burden of public incentives—primarily compounding sales and use tax exemptions—revealing that the public cost per permanent data-centre job routinely exceeds $1 million to $2 million, vastly underperforming equal-investment comparators like advanced manufacturing and automated logistics. Third, ex-post audits reveal a systemic enforcement gap: developers frequently miss promised job targets, and state agencies routinely fail to enforce clawback provisions. In response, local municipalities are increasingly bypassing state-level tax abatements in favour of legally binding Community-Benefit Agreements (CBAs) and Project Labor Agreements (PLAs) to enforce local hiring, wage minimums, and environmental standards.

## 2) Assessment of the Current Search Approach and its Coverage
The systematic search utilised a batched, multi-slice protocol to prevent algorithmic collapse, ensuring high-recall retrieval across discrete thematic axes (workforce dynamics, fiscal architecture, and ex-post accountability). This approach successfully captured a robust mix of peer-reviewed econometrics, official government audits (e.g., JLARC Virginia, Minnesota LBO), and grey-literature compliance ledgers (e.g., Good Jobs First). 

Initial search iterations revealed a gap in standardised cross-sector comparators and international audits. To address this, a two-hop structural snowballing protocol was executed. Backward snowballing traced foundational urban economics and megaproject management theories (e.g., agglomeration failures, optimism bias), while forward snowballing captured recent empirical evaluations of local welfare impacts, spatial competition for tax breaks, and energy-transition comparators. This expanded the coverage from isolated state audits to a systemic critique of how extractive digital capital interacts with municipal finance, GASB 77 compliance, and local labour markets.

## 7) Methods / Approaches Synthesis
The methodological landscape for evaluating data-centre economic impacts is sharply divided. Industry-sponsored assessments predominantly rely on static Input-Output (I-O) models (e.g., IMPLAN, RIMS II). These models mechanically assume that capital expenditure linearly generates indirect and induced local jobs based on historical manufacturing averages. However, academic and audit literature heavily critiques this approach due to "economic leakage"—up to 80% of data-centre capex is spent on imported, highly specialised hardware (GPUs, cooling loops), meaning the capital instantly bypasses the local economy. 

Consequently, rigorous evaluations have shifted toward causal econometric frameworks, such as Difference-in-Differences (DiD) and Double Machine Learning models, to measure net local welfare effects. These models control for pre-existing growth trends and spatial competition, consistently demonstrating that data centres produce negligible net-new local employment and often degrade local welfare for non-tech incumbent residents by driving up housing and utility costs without providing commensurate wage premiums.

## 8) Applications, Outcomes, and Adoption Synthesis
*   **Labour Leakage & Construction Dynamics:** While data centres generate temporary construction surges (800–1,200 workers), the highly specialised mechanical, electrical, and plumbing (MEP) requirements necessitate a mobile, transient workforce. Local labour pools rarely capture the highest-paying construction wages.
*   **Fiscal Outcomes & Cost Per Job:** The application of compounding sales and use tax exemptions to continuously refreshed server equipment creates a perpetual drain on municipal revenues. The public cost per permanent data-centre job averages $1.95 million, with extreme outliers reaching $6.4 million to $11 million per job.
*   **Cross-Sector Comparators:** When normalised by capital investment, data centres are highly inefficient. Data centres generate approximately 3 jobs per $100 million invested. In contrast, advanced semiconductor fabs generate ~10.7 jobs per $100 million, and traditional manufacturing generates ~168 jobs per $100 million. Automated logistics hubs also vastly outperform data centres in spatial employment density (jobs per square foot).
*   **Adoption of Corrective Mechanisms:** Due to state-level enforcement failures, local municipalities are increasingly adopting Community-Benefit Agreements (CBAs) and Project Labor Agreements (PLAs). These legally binding instruments mandate local hiring quotas, fund registered apprenticeships, and enforce environmental guardrails (e.g., water usage limits) with strict financial penalties.

## 9) Quality, Governance, Risk, and Limitations Synthesis
*   **Risk of Bias:** Evidence quality is highly stratified. Industry-commissioned economic impact reports carry a high risk of bias due to unadjusted I-O multipliers and the conflation of temporary construction jobs with permanent operational FTEs. Peer-reviewed econometrics and state legislative audits carry a low risk of bias.
*   **Governance & Transparency:** A systemic governance failure exists regarding GASB Statement No. 77 compliance. Many municipalities actively hide the true fiscal cost of data-centre tax abatements from taxpayers, which can perversely result in higher municipal borrowing costs (a "double penalty" for taxpayers).
*   **Enforcement Risk:** Clawback provisions in economic development agreements are frequently discretionary, poorly drafted, or circumvented by corporate restructuring. Politicians often prioritise the political signaling of "megadeals" over rigorous ex-post enforcement.
*   **Limitations:** The primary limitation in the evidence base is the opacity created by corporate Non-Disclosure Agreements (NDAs) during the site-selection phase, which prevents independent ex-ante fiscal forecasting.

## 11) Research Gaps and Next Steps
1.  **International Ex-Post Audits:** The current evidence base is heavily skewed toward US state comptroller reports. Future research must target parliamentary inquiries and audit data from other major hyperscale markets (e.g., Australia, Ireland, Germany).
2.  **Standardised Cross-Sector Comparators:** Further empirical work is needed to consolidate job-to-capex and job-to-sqm ratios across a wider array of industrial-zoned assets to refine the "equal-investment benchmark."
3.  **Job Quality and Subcontracting Breakdowns:** Ex-post audits must explicitly separate permanent operational jobs into direct, high-wage corporate employees versus low-wage, subcontracted/vendor FTEs (e.g., security, landscaping).
4.  **Causal Net Employment Econometrics:** Expansion of Difference-in-Differences methodologies to isolate the causal local employment change attributable to data-centre entry, strictly filtering out broad "cloud ecosystem" jobs.
