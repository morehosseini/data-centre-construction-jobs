# Final Evidence Dossier: Renewable Energy Jobs per Dollar and Data-Centre Off-Site Prefabrication Labour

## 1) Executive Summary
The global transition toward a decarbonised and digitised economy is driven by two massive vectors of capital expenditure: renewable energy generation and hyperscale digital infrastructure (data centres). Despite both requiring billions in upfront capital and gigawatt-scale grid integrations, their socio-economic employment propositions are fundamentally divergent. 

Renewable energy and energy efficiency investments yield exceptionally high, geographically bound employment multipliers. The academic consensus establishes a "Green vs. Brown" multiplier, demonstrating that clean energy investments generate approximately 7.49 jobs per US$1 million invested, compared to just 2.65 jobs for fossil fuels. Energy efficiency retrofits offer the highest local yield (up to 15–22 jobs per million) because the labour cannot be offshored.

Conversely, the data centre sector is undergoing rapid industrialisation to combat acute skilled labour shortages and compress delivery schedules. Developers are aggressively adopting Prefabrication, Preassembly, Modularization, and Off-site Fabrication (PPMOF). While vendor literature boasts of 89% to 98% reductions in on-site labour, empirical evidence reveals this is an "optical illusion" of job elimination. The net total labour reduction (site plus factory) is only 10% to 15%; the vast majority of the labour is simply geographically dislocated from the host community to remote, centralised manufacturing hubs. Consequently, the local economic multiplier of data-centre capital expenditure is severely diminished, fundamentally altering the opportunity cost for host communities.

## 2) Assessment of the Current Search Approach and its Coverage
The search methodology employed a rigorous, multi-hop structural snowballing protocol to bridge macroeconomic energy modelling and microeconomic construction management. 
*   **Initial Phase:** Captured foundational macroeconomic multipliers (e.g., Wei et al., NREL JEDI) and Australian-specific bottom-up workforce projections (AEMO ISP, UTS ISF).
*   **Snowballing Rounds 1 & 2:** Traced the theoretical parents of off-site construction (MiC, PPMOF) and captured modern empirical updates on the "CapEx Premium" paradox of modular construction.
*   **Snowballing Round 3:** Broadened the scope to Regional Economics and Industrial Engineering to successfully uncover the "missing denominator" (exact factory-to-site labour hour ratios) and quantify local economic leakage. 

The coverage is highly robust for energy multipliers and modular schedule compression, though it highlights a persistent, industry-wide gap in transparent, project-level total labour audits for proprietary hyperscale data centres.

## 7) Methods / Approaches Synthesis
The quantification of infrastructure employment relies on a bifurcated methodological landscape:
1.  **Macroeconomic Input-Output (I-O) & CGE Models:** Used to calculate direct, indirect, and induced jobs per million dollars (e.g., NREL JEDI, IMPLAN). These models excel at capturing supply chain ripples but struggle with dynamic price elasticities and geographic leakage if local manufacturing is absent.
2.  **Analytical Process (Bottom-Up) Models:** Used by AEMO and UTS ISF to generate highly specific job-years per MW. This normalises employment over asset lifespans and capacity factors (the WPK method), cleanly separating transient construction from permanent O&M.
3.  **Microeconomic Project Tracking (BIM/ERP):** Used in construction management to track exact labour hours for modular MEP. Advanced studies utilise AHP-Entropy-Fuzzy evaluation to benchmark modularity against traditional stick-built methods.

## 8) Applications, Outcomes, and Adoption Synthesis
*   **Renewable Energy & Efficiency:** Acts as a massive, geographically bound economic stimulus. Energy efficiency retrofits yield the highest local outcomes (up to 15 jobs/$1M) because the labour (HVAC, weatherisation) is strictly non-offshorable.
*   **Data Centre Modularisation:** Adopted universally by hyperscalers to bypass local trade shortages and achieve 20-50% schedule compression. While on-site labour is reduced by up to 89%, 70% of direct task labour is simply shifted to off-site factories. Operational employment is exceptionally low (0.15 to 0.20 FTEs per MW).

## 9) Quality, Governance, Risk, and Limitations Synthesis
*   **The "Optical Illusion" of Labour Elimination:** Vendor literature presents a high risk of bias by promoting on-site labour reductions without quantifying the corresponding factory labour increases.
*   **Geographic Leakage:** Green multipliers are vulnerable to leakage if a region lacks manufacturing capacity (e.g., 41% of global renewable jobs are in China). Similarly, data centre MEP jobs leak to centralised global vendor factories.
*   **The CapEx Premium Paradox:** Modular construction does not inherently save money. Due to complex logistics, crating, and factory overhead, it frequently carries a 20-30% CapEx premium unless massive, repeatable scale is achieved. This inflates the denominator, further suppressing the "jobs per million" metric for data centres.

## 11) Research Gaps and Next Steps
1.  **Total Labour Audits for Hyperscale DCs:** There is a critical need for independent, peer-reviewed audits that track the *complete* lifecycle labour (factory + site) of proprietary hyperscale data centres.
2.  **Standardised Digital Infrastructure Multipliers:** The industry requires updated jobs-per-$1M metrics that account for recent CapEx inflation driven by AI-density requirements and liquid cooling.
3.  **Equal-Investment Comparators:** Future research must benchmark data centre employment density against other capital-intensive, industrial-zoned assets, specifically advanced semiconductor fabrication plants and automated logistics fulfilment centres.
