# Final Evidence Dossier: Gigafactory and Semiconductor Fab Construction and Operations Jobs per Capex

## 1) Executive Summary
The global transition toward resilient domestic supply chains and advanced computing has catalyzed a capital expenditure (capex) supercycle in advanced manufacturing. However, regional economic models frequently mischaracterize the labor dynamics of these multi-billion-dollar megaprojects by conflating temporary construction spikes with permanent operational roles, and by failing to disaggregate physical building capex from imported manufacturing equipment capex. 

This dossier synthesizes empirical evidence across semiconductor fabrication plants (fabs) and battery gigafactories to establish accurate labor density metrics (jobs per $1 billion capex). The evidence demonstrates a stark divergence: leading-edge semiconductor fabs exhibit extremely low operational labor density (~90–110 jobs per $1B capex) due to a ~30/70 building-to-equipment capex split, where the vast majority of capital leaks to global equipment vendors. Conversely, battery gigafactories exhibit higher labor density (~800–1,100 jobs per $1B capex) driven by a more balanced ~44/56 capex split and more labor-intensive cell assembly processes. Furthermore, the construction phase of these assets creates a severe "workforce cliff," with up to 75–80% of peak construction labor imported from out-of-region, severely dampening the localized economic multipliers frequently touted in ex-ante projections.

## 2) Assessment of the Current Search Approach and its Coverage
The search utilized a structural snowballing methodology (2-hop protocol). Backward snowballing traced the theoretical justifications for megaproject subsidies to foundational regional economics (e.g., agglomeration spillovers, the "Big Push" theory, and megaproject optimism bias). Forward snowballing applied these frameworks to the modern CHIPS Act and Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) era. 

**Coverage Strengths:** The search successfully captured high-fidelity *ex-ante* federal Environmental Assessments (EAs) (e.g., U.S. DOE and DOC EAs for TSMC, Intel, BlueOval SK, Panasonic) and rare *ex-post* state audits (e.g., Nevada GOED Tesla audit, Wisconsin LAB Foxconn audit). It robustly covers the theoretical flaws of Input-Output (I-O) models when applied to globally fragmented supply chains.
**Coverage Limitations:** The literature remains heavily skewed toward *ex-ante* projections. There is a severe lack of *ex-post* independent audits verifying realized steady-state operational headcounts for newly completed EUV fabs, and limited empirical tracking of how massive construction cost overruns alter the final jobs-per-billion metric.

## 7) Methods / Approaches Synthesis
Evaluating the employment impacts of advanced manufacturing relies on a spectrum of econometric models, each with distinct strengths and critical flaws:
*   **Input-Output (I-O) Models (IMPLAN, RIMS II):** Ubiquitous for initial grant justifications. *Flaw:* They assume fixed proportions and frequently fail to subtract massive import leakages (e.g., $200M EUV scanners imported from Europe), resulting in catastrophic overestimations of local indirect/induced jobs.
*   **Dynamic General Equilibrium (REMI):** Accounts for price changes, wage inflation, and labor migration. Better for modeling the spatial strain of transient construction workers, but still vulnerable to monolithic capex assumptions.
*   **Lifecycle Assessment (LCA) Employment Factors:** Scales jobs by output capacity rather than volatile capital costs (e.g., 140-180 jobs per GWh for batteries). Highly reliable for sector forecasting.
*   **Ex-Post Statutory Audits (Agreed Upon Procedures):** The gold standard. Direct empirical verification of realized employment, local residency capture, and exact capex deployment (e.g., Nevada GOED audits).

## 8) Applications, Outcomes, and Adoption Synthesis
Empirical data reveals distinct labor density profiles across asset classes based on their automation levels and capex splits:
*   **Semiconductor Fabs (Leading-Edge):** Hyper-capital intensive. Capex split is roughly 30% buildings / 70% equipment. Operational labor density is exceptionally low: **~90 to 110 direct jobs per $1 Billion capex** (e.g., TSMC AZ: 92 jobs/$1B; Intel OH: 107 jobs/$1B).
*   **Battery Gigafactories (Final Assembly):** More labor-intensive. Capex split is roughly 44% buildings / 56% equipment. Operational labor density is significantly higher: **~800 to 1,167 direct jobs per $1 Billion capex** (e.g., BlueOval SK: 862 jobs/$1B; Tesla NV: 1,167 jobs/$1B).
*   **Mid-Stream Supply Chain:** Legacy fabs and advanced packaging (e.g., Absolics) yield ~400 to 1,333 jobs/$1B. Battery components (anodes/separators) yield ~500 to 630 jobs/$1B.
*   **Hyperscale Data Centers (Comparator):** The extreme low-density benchmark, yielding only **~40 to 80 jobs per $1 Billion capex**, functioning more as automated computing environments than traditional employers.

## 9) Quality, Governance, Risk, and Limitations Synthesis
*   **The Workforce Cliff & Labor Leakage:** Megaprojects act as "labor vacuums." Local markets cannot absorb the 5,000–10,000 peak headcount required. Consequently, up to 75–80% of peak construction labor is imported (transient). This causes acute localized inflation in housing and services without permanently expanding the local tax base.
*   **Megaproject Optimism Bias:** *Ex-ante* models systematically overestimate benefits. The Foxconn Wisconsin project (projected 13,000 jobs, realized 216 eligible employees) exemplifies the catastrophic failure of un-audited projections.
*   **Metric Conflation:** A pervasive methodological risk is the conflation of "cumulative individual hires" or "construction person-years" with "peak concurrent headcount," obscuring the true temporal strain on municipal infrastructure.
*   **Governance:** Mitigating these risks requires strict Community Benefit Agreements (CBAs) and statutory residency mandates (e.g., Nevada's requirement that 50% of workers be state residents to qualify for abatements).

## 11) Research Gaps and Next Steps
*   **Gaps Identified:** 
    1) Lack of *ex-post* financial audits confirming the realized capex split for newly completed EUV fabs. 
    2) Insufficient empirical tracking of how multi-billion-dollar cost overruns (e.g., Intel Ohio escalating from $20B to $28B) alter local construction job-years versus simply reflecting inflated imported equipment costs. 
    3) Over-reliance on static I-O multipliers that fail to subtract global supply chain leakage.
*   **Next Steps:** 
    1) Conduct longitudinal audits of CHIPS Act facilities 3-5 years post "tool-in" to verify steady-state operational headcounts. 
    2) Standardize NEPA EA labor reporting to strictly separate peak headcount from person-years. 
    3) Develop dynamic spatial models that explicitly discount imported SME (Semiconductor Manufacturing Equipment) from local multiplier calculations.
