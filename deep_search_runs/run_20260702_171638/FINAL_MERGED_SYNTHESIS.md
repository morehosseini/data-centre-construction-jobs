# Final Evidence Dossier: Hospital, School, and Housing Construction and Operations Jobs per Capex

## 1) Executive Summary
As advanced economies navigate severe social infrastructure deficits alongside the rapid spatial encroachment of hyper-capital-intensive digital and logistics assets, evaluating the opportunity cost of capital is paramount. This dossier synthesizes the macroeconomic evidence contrasting the employment densities of social infrastructure (hospitals, schools, affordable housing) against automated industrial assets (hyperscale data centres, gigafactories, automated logistics). 

The evidence demonstrates a profound structural divergence in capital-to-labour ratios. While automated industrial assets generate transient construction employment characterized by extreme geographic supply-chain leakage and near-zero operational density, social infrastructure functions as a permanent economic anchor. Hospitals and educational facilities yield compounding, highly localized operational employment that drives regional agglomeration (the "Moretti effect"). Furthermore, residential construction acts as a massive domestic supply-chain shock, unburdening household incomes and driving induced economic growth. In capacity-constrained markets, allocating finite land and electrical grid capacity to low-employment industrial mega-projects actively crowds out the labour and materials urgently required for civic development, fundamentally altering a municipality's long-term socioeconomic trajectory.

## 2) Assessment of the Current Search Approach and its Coverage
The search strategy utilized a multi-layered, three-hop structural snowballing protocol to move beyond basic, often-flawed Input-Output (I-O) multipliers. 
*   **Initial Phase:** Targeted baseline construction multipliers and operational densities across asset classes, capturing foundational metrics from government treasuries and industry bodies.
*   **Hop 1 & 2 (Urban Economics & Spatial Friction):** Broadened the lens to capture the spatial friction of construction labour and the agglomeration power of operational anchors. This successfully integrated peer-reviewed evidence on spatial leakage (demonstrating local areas capture only ~25% of construction employment benefits) and the "Moretti effect" of local job multipliers.
*   **Hop 3 (Knowledge Spillovers & Green Premiums):** Targeted the operational spillovers of knowledge infrastructure and the labour intensity of green civic buildings. 

**Coverage Assessment:** The approach provides robust coverage of advanced economies (US, UK, Australia) and successfully contrasts gross vendor claims with net causal local employment. However, coverage remains limited regarding standardized operational densities for highly automated logistics hubs and specific mixed-use civic facilities, which lack the rigid benchmarking seen in healthcare (e.g., FTE per bed).

## 5) Inclusion and Exclusion Criteria

**Inclusion Criteria:**
*   Studies reporting normalized employment metrics (e.g., jobs per $1M capex, FTE per bed, FTE per sqm).
*   Empirical analyses distinguishing between direct, indirect (Type I), and induced (Type II) employment.
*   Research addressing spatial leakage, crowding-out effects, or macroeconomic capacity constraints in advanced economies.
*   Published between 2015 and 2026 (with exceptions for foundational macroeconomic theory).

**Exclusion Criteria:**
*   Vendor or industry reports that conflate peak construction headcount with annualized FTE without transparent denominators.
*   Studies claiming broad IT/software ecosystem jobs as a direct result of a data centre development without proving a causal, localized operational link.
*   Input-Output models that fail to acknowledge displacement or deadweight loss in capacity-constrained markets.

## 7) Methods / Approaches Synthesis
The evaluation of infrastructure employment relies on a spectrum of models. **Input-Output (I-O) Analysis** is the standard for local business cases, tracing fixed-proportion supply chain linkages to calculate direct, indirect (Type I), and induced (Type II) jobs. However, I-O models assume infinite supply elasticity and routinely overestimate net job creation. 

To correct this, advanced economic evaluations utilize **Computable General Equilibrium (CGE)** models, which enforce budget constraints and price clearing mechanisms, accurately reflecting the *crowding-out effect* where public projects displace private labour. Furthermore, the **Moretti Local Multiplier** framework is critical for operational analysis, proving that adding highly skilled operational jobs (e.g., hospital clinicians) disproportionately expands local non-tradable employment (retail, services). Finally, spatial econometrics reveals **spatial leakage**, demonstrating that highly mobile construction labour often repatriates wages outside the host municipality, meaning local areas capture only a fraction (~25%) of the modelled construction employment.

## 8) Applications, Outcomes, and Adoption Synthesis
*   **Healthcare:** Hospitals are the highest-density operational hubs. Construction yields ~3.5 direct jobs per $1M capex. Operationally, they require 4.0 to 5.5 permanent FTE per commissioned bed (scaling higher for ICUs).
*   **Education & Civic:** School construction yields ~4.2 direct jobs per $1M capex. Operationally, universities generate a 1:1 multiplier (1 direct FTE supports 1 indirect FTE) and act as agglomeration engines. Civic spaces operate at high densities (e.g., 1 FTE per 30-40 sqm).
*   **Housing:** Residential construction acts as a massive domestic supply-chain shock, supporting ~9 total jobs per $1M output. Operationally, affordable housing unburdens household incomes, reducing discretionary drag by up to 15% and driving induced local consumption.
*   **Industrial Comparators (Data Centres/Gigafactories):** These assets suffer from extreme spatial leakage, as up to 70% of capex is spent on imported proprietary hardware. Construction yields are diluted (0.37 to 1.5 jobs per $1M capex), and operational densities are near zero (e.g., 50-100 jobs for a $1B facility).

## 9) Quality, Governance, Risk, and Limitations Synthesis
The primary risk in infrastructure economic forecasting is **denominator degradation**. As inflation escalates the cost of materials and specialized robotics, $1 million buys significantly fewer labour hours today than a decade ago, rendering historical "jobs per million" benchmarks obsolete if not adjusted. 

Additionally, the **fallacy of gross job creation** plagues vendor-led business cases. In capacity-constrained markets (e.g., Australia, UK, US), injecting billions into mega-projects does not create new aggregate jobs; it displaces them. The crowding-out effect means that dedicating scarce trades to a hyperscale data centre actively starves the residential and civic sectors of the labour required to alleviate housing and healthcare crises. Finally, the aggressive inflation of induced employment via unadjusted respending multipliers (often claiming 4 induced jobs per direct job, rather than the academically supported 0.5) fundamentally distorts cross-asset comparisons.

## 11) Research Gaps and Next Steps
1.  **Standardized Industrial Comparators:** There is a distinct lack of normalized, source-backed metrics for modern, highly automated logistics hubs and gigafactories to serve as direct mid-point comparators against social infrastructure.
2.  **Advanced Economy Crowding-Out Evidence:** While theoretical consensus exists, there is insufficient granular empirical literature quantifying the exact crowding-out effect of tech mega-projects on private residential construction labour in capacity-constrained advanced economies.
3.  **Civic Asset Operational Ratios:** A lack of authoritative post-occupancy evaluations providing standardized operational employment density metrics (FTE per sqm) for modern, mixed-use civic and public realm infrastructure.
4.  **Next Steps:** Execute the batched Scopus/WoS queries targeting the specific operational densities of automated warehousing and execute targeted grey-literature searches for recent (2023-2026) municipal post-occupancy evaluations of civic hubs.
